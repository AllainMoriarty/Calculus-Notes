> **The derivative of $ x^n $ is $ nx^{n-1} $ â€” the most useful rule in calculus.**

## ðŸ”¢ Derivative of Powers: $ f(x) = x^n $

We begin with **positive integers** $ n $, then extend to **all real exponents**.

### Example: $ f(x) = x^3 $
Compute the derivative using the limit definition:
$$
\Delta f = f(x + h) - f(x) = (x + h)^3 - x^3 = x^3 + 3x^2h + 3xh^2 + h^3 - x^3
$$
$$
\frac{\Delta f}{h} = 3x^2 + 3xh + h^2 \quad \xrightarrow{h \to 0} \quad f'(x) = 3x^2
$$

âœ… **Pattern**:  
- $ x^2 \to 2x $  
- $ x^3 \to 3x^2 $  
- $ x^4 \to 4x^3 $

## ðŸ“ General Rule: Power Rule

> **The derivative of $ x^n $ is $ nx^{n-1} $**  
> for any real number $ n $ (integer, fraction, negative, irrational).

### Binomial Expansion (for integer $ n $):
$$
(x + h)^n = x^n + nx^{n-1}h + \binom{n}{2}x^{n-2}h^2 + \cdots + h^n
$$
Subtract $ x^n $, divide by $ h $:
$$
\frac{(x + h)^n - x^n}{h} = nx^{n-1} + \binom{n}{2}x^{n-2}h + \cdots + h^{n-1}
$$
Take limit $ h \to 0 $: all terms with $ h $ vanish â†’ only **$ nx^{n-1} $** remains.

âœ… **Pascalâ€™s Triangle** gives binomial coefficients:
```
n=0:        1
n=1:      1   1
n=2:    1   2   1
n=3:  1   3   3   1
n=4: 1   4   6   4   1
```

## ðŸ“ˆ Power Rule for All Exponents

The rule $ \frac{d}{dx}(x^n) = nx^{n-1} $ works even when $ n $ is **not** an integer:

| Function $ f(x) $ | Derivative $ f'(x) $ |
|--------------------|------------------------|
| $ x^{10} $       | $ 10x^9 $            |
| $ x^{2.2} $      | $ 2.2x^{1.2} $       |
| $ x^{-1} = \frac{1}{x} $ | $ -x^{-2} = -\frac{1}{x^2} $ |
| $ x^{-2} = \frac{1}{x^2} $ | $ -2x^{-3} = -\frac{2}{x^3} $ |
| $ x^{1/2} = \sqrt{x} $ | $ \frac{1}{2}x^{-1/2} = \frac{1}{2\sqrt{x}} $ |
| $ x^{-1/2} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{x}} $ | $ -\frac{1}{2}x^{-3/2} = -\frac{1}{2x\sqrt{x}} $ |

> ðŸ’¡ **Key idea**: Bring down the exponent, reduce power by 1.

## âž•âž– Derivatives of Polynomials

A **polynomial** is a sum of power functions:
$$
f(x) = a_nx^n + a_{n-1}x^{n-1} + \cdots + a_1x + a_0
$$

### Two Key Rules:

1. **Constant Multiple Rule**:  
   $$
   \frac{d}{dx}[c \cdot f(x)] = c \cdot f'(x)
   $$

2. **Sum Rule**:  
   $$
   \frac{d}{dx}[f(x) + g(x)] = f'(x) + g'(x)
   $$

âœ… **Derivative of any polynomial**: Differentiate term by term.

### Examples:

- $ f(x) = 9 + 2x - x^5 $  
  $ f'(x) = 0 + 2 - 5x^4 = 2 - 5x^4 $

- $ f(x) = 6x^3 + \frac{1}{2}x^2 $  
  $ f'(x) = 18x^2 + x $

- $ f(x) = 6x^3 - \frac{1}{2}x^2 $  
  $ f'(x) = 18x^2 - x $

> âš ï¸ **Derivative of a constant is zero** â†’ constants disappear in differentiation (but reappear in integration!).

## ðŸ” Differential Equations: Reverse Process

Given **derivative**, find **function**.

### Example:
- If $ \frac{dy}{dx} = 3x^2 $, then $ y = x^3 + C $
- If $ \frac{dy}{dx} = 2x + 1 $, then $ y = x^2 + x + C $

> ðŸ’¡ **Constant $ C $** = unknown starting value (e.g., initial position)

### Real-World Example: AIDS Epidemic (1989)
- Data fit **cubic polynomial**:  
  $$
  y = 174.6(t - 1981.2)^3 + 340
  $$
- Derivative: $ \frac{dy}{dt} = 3 \cdot 174.6(t - 1981.2)^2 $
- Not exponential $ (e^t) $, but **cubic growth** â†’ suggests different dynamics

âœ… **Differential equations mix $ y $ and $ \frac{dy}{dx} $**:
- $ \frac{dy}{dx} = 3y/x $ â†’ solved by $ y = x^3 $
- $ \frac{dy}{dx} = 3y^{2/3} $ â†’ also solved by $ y = x^3 $

## ðŸ’° Economics: Marginal Cost & Elasticity

### Marginal Cost
- **Average cost** = total cost / quantity â†’ describes past  
- **Marginal cost** = derivative of cost â†’ predicts future

Example:  
- Cost = $ x^2 $ â†’ Average cost = $ x $, Marginal cost = $ 2x $
- Marginal > Average â†’ increasing returns

### Elasticity of Demand

Measures **relative change** (percentage), independent of units.

**Definition**:  
$$
E(x) = \frac{dy/dx}{y/x} = \left( \frac{dy}{dx} \right) \cdot \left( \frac{x}{y} \right)
$$

- $ E = $ (marginal) / (average)
- Dimensionless â†’ same in dollars or pesos

#### Interpretation:
- $ |E| > 1 $: **Elastic** â†’ demand sensitive to price
- $ |E| < 1 $: **Inelastic** â†’ demand not sensitive
- $ E = -1 $: **Unit elastic** (spending = constant)

---

### Example 4: $ y = \frac{c}{x} $ (Demand)
- $ \frac{dy}{dx} = -\frac{c}{x^2} $
- $ \frac{y}{x} = \frac{c}{x^2} $
- Elasticity:  
  $$
  E = \frac{-c/x^2}{c/x^2} = -1
  $$

âœ… **Constant spending**: $ x \cdot y = c $

---

### Power Functions & Constant Elasticity

If $ y = c x^n $, then:
$$
\frac{dy}{dx} = c n x^{n-1}, \quad \frac{y}{x} = c x^{n-1}
\Rightarrow E = \frac{c n x^{n-1}}{c x^{n-1}} = n
$$

âœ… **Power functions have constant elasticity = exponent**

| Function | Elasticity |
|---------|-----------|
| $ y = \frac{1}{x} = x^{-1} $ | $ E = -1 $ |
| $ y = \frac{1}{\sqrt{x}} = x^{-1/2} $ | $ E = -1/2 $ (inelastic) |
| $ y = \frac{1}{x^3} = x^{-3} $ | $ E = -3 $ (elastic) |

---

### Supply Elasticity

- **Supply**: $ y = cx $ â†’ $ E = 1 $
- **Perfectly inelastic**: $ y = c $ (fixed supply) â†’ $ E = 0 $
- **Perfectly elastic**: horizontal supply â†’ $ E = \infty $

### Income Elasticity

Measures how demand changes with income:
$$
E = \frac{dy/dI}{y/I}
$$

- **Luxury**: $ E > 1 $ â†’ demand grows faster than income
- **Necessity**: $ E < 1 $ â†’ demand grows slower

#### Example: Savings
- Income = \$10,000 â†’ Savings = \$500
- Income elasticity $ E = 2 $
- So savings = $ y = cI^2 $
- Solve: $ 500 = c(10{,}000)^2 \Rightarrow c = 5 \times 10^{-6} $
- Marginal savings = $ \frac{dy}{dI} = 2cI = 10 \times 10^{-6} \times 10^4 = 0.1 $
- **Save 10Â¢ of next dollar** (marginal), even though average is 5%
